Development of the SpecMAT active target


During my PhD I have developed and built a state-of-the-art detector - the SpecMAT active target. At this moment (2021), SpecMAT is a unique detector that combines the active target technology with high-resolution scintillation detectors capable of operating in a strong magnetic field. The SpecMAT was built to study atomic nuclei with single-nucleon transfer reactions. The active target can acquire three-dimensional tracks of light reaction products and work in coincidence with the scintillation array sensitive to gamma-rays emitted by the heavy reaction products. Such a combination of detectors sensitive to charged particles and gamma-rays in one setup allows obtaining a maximal amount of experimental observables from a reaction.

Construction of the scintillation array


Prototyping and testing scintillation gamma-ray detectors
I tested various scintillation crystals and novel SiPM photomultipliers coupled to digital readout electronics (including tests in strong magnetic fields) for the future detector array of the SpecMAT project.

These tests and R&D of the detectors lead to the final design and production of the detectors by SCIONIX.

Geant4 simulations
Before designing and producing the scintillation array, I have performed detailed simulations of the gamma-ray detection efficiency of stand-alone scintillation detectors and detectors arranged into arrays using the Geant4 Monte Carlo simulation toolkit (in C++ programming language).


My Geant4 models and ROOT analysis codes can be found on GitHub

Generative design of the array holder in Fusion360
This part of the project includes the reduction of the detector support weight based on finite element stress analysis.


The designed parts were 3D printed using the SLS technique.

Construction of the active target


Simulation of electrons tracks in an electric field with SIMION.
Optimisation of the TPC's filed cage geometry is highly important for identifying charged particle tracks in the detector. I made simulations of electrons trajectories in a TPC for various field cage geometries that allowed me to choose an optimal configuration.

COMSOL Multiphysics simulations of electric field homoginiety
More advanced and systematic simulations of the electric field in the TPC field cage I have made with COMSOL Multiphysics using geometries created in CAD software.


This electric field simulation helped to define a preliminary shape of the gas chamber. However, the finalisation of the chamber design requires further mechanical simulations.

COMSOL Multiphysics simulations of stress distribution
To define the final shape of the gas chamber, I simulated stress distribution in the chamber under vacuum and overpressure conditions. These simulations were also performed in COMSOL with geometry inputs from Autodesk Inventor.

Mechanical design and construction of the detector components
I refined the design of the gas chamber based on the FEA simulation outputs. Based on my design, the gas chamber of the detector was produced by an external company.

Characterisation of the active target
To operate the detector in an environment with a reduced amount of dust, I upgraded the cleanroom of the laboratory to Class 100 (ISO 5). The assembling of the gaseous detector in the cleanroom is shown below.

Integrating all the built systems and detectors, I assembled the SpecMAT active target and characterised it with a measurement of decay products of 241Am. Development of a scintillation array for the ISOLDE Solenoidal Spectrometer


In 2022 I designed a new scintillation array for an upgrade of the ISOLDE Solenoidal Spectrometer. The new detector array reuses the scintillation detectors of SpecMAT and is fully optimised for operation in a strong magnetic field. This scintillation array is used for gamma-ray spectroscopy in coincidence with charged particles, which are detected in the silicon array of ISS. The array was characterised at ISOLDE, CERN in November 2022. The mechanics of the array was partly machined in Sweden in collaboration with A. Heinz.


Upgrade of ACTAR and ACTAR-DEMONSTRATOR active targets


Double-Sided Silicon Strip Detectors for the ACTAR-Demonstrator active target (INFN-LNL/LNS, Legnaro/Catania, Italy)
As a side project during my PhD, I contributed to the ACTAR-Demonstrator and the ACTAR active target projects with the design and development of the auxiliary Double-Sided Silicon Strip Detectors (DSSSD) arrays and front-end readout electronics for DSSSDs with its further integration with General Electronics for Timpe projection chambers (GET).

Double-sided Silicon Strip Detector arrays for the ACTAR active target (GANIL, Caen, France)
ACTAR-Demonstrator became an incredible learning platform where our research group and I gained experience in integrating DSSSD detectors into an active target. With the new knowledge, we produced more advanced MK2 Silicon arrays for ACTAR.


Characterisation of a novel plastic scintillation material


At the University of Jyväskylä, I tested the performance of a nowel plastic scintillation material on gamma-neutron discrimination properties. The obtained results I compared with the performance of a well known BC-501A liquid scintillator.

Calibration of a low-background high-purity germanium detector


One of my summer internship tasks was a precise calibration of a High-Purity Germanium (HPGe) detector efficiency. The efficiency of this detector is important for accurate activity measurements of weak radioactive samples. A series of measurements with various gamma-ray sources were done to make this calibration, and a calibration device for precise positioning of sources was designed.
